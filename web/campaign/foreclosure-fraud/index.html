What Is Foreclosure Fraud?

Foreclosure fraud happens when a bank or mortgage servicer uses illegal, deceptive, or procedurally improper methods to take your home. Courts across the country have ruled that many foreclosures from the past two decades were legally void — meaning the bank never had the right to foreclose in the first place.

Important: The statute of limitations on fraud claims varies by state — typically 2 to 6 years. However, courts in many states have ruled that void foreclosure judgments can be challenged at any time, because a void judgment has no legal force. Don't assume it's too late. Get a legal review.

✍️

Robo-Signing

Bank employees signed thousands of foreclosure documents per day without reading them — falsely swearing under oath they had personal knowledge of each case. Courts have called this fraud.

📜

Chain of Title Fraud

To foreclose legally, a bank must prove it owns your loan. Many cannot — the mortgage was sold multiple times through MERS and other channels, leaving the chain of ownership broken or forged.

🚫

Loan Modification Fraud

Your servicer accepted trial modification payments, then foreclosed anyway — often without proper notice, or while your modification application was still under active review (illegal dual tracking).

📋

Improper Procedures

The law requires specific notices, timelines, and steps before a foreclosure can proceed. Servicers routinely shortcut these requirements — cutting the 90-day cure period to 33 days, for example.

💰

Illegal Fees & Charges

Mortgage servicers have been caught charging illegal fees for property monitoring and forced-place insurance, and using disputed interest calculation methods that cost homeowners tens of thousands.

🏦

MERS & Void Assignments

Mortgages routed through MERS were assigned in legally questionable ways. Courts have ruled certain assignments void — meaning the foreclosing bank lacked legal standing to take your home.

Foreclosure fraud didn't end in 2012. Courts and regulators continue to find and punish illegal bank behavior — which means victims continue to come forward with valid claims.
